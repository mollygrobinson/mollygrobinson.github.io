The NFL Draft has come to an end and the league is finalizing schedules, meaning football fans and fantasy football junkies alike are anticipating the start of the season and drafting their dream fantasy rosters.

Fantasy football is meant to be taken very seriously, as fans will soon closely monitor schedules, plan what players they want to draft, and create their own leagues.

With the fantasy draft, finding key receivers is crucial to a team’s success. There’s currently a plethora of incredible wide receivers, but don’t neglect this new very talented draft class.

So… who should you draft? Let’s look at some different metrics.

We’re going to take a look at PPR, or points per reception, total receptions, receiving yards, yards per reception and the offenses’ success.

library(tidyverse)
library(tidymodels)
library(cfbfastR)
library(janitor)
library(bonsai)
library(gt)
library(ggplot2)
set.seed(1001)


wrstats <- read_csv("https://mattwaite.github.io/sportsdatafiles/wrdraftedstatswithratingsfixed.csv")

wrselected <- wrstats %>%
  select(
    name,
    year,
    college_team,
    nfl_team,
    overall,
    total_receptions,
    total_yards,
    total_ypr,
    rating,
    FantPt
  ) %>% na.omit()

player_split <- initial_split(wrselected, prop = .8)

player_train <- training(player_split)

player_test <- testing(player_split)

player_recipe <-
  recipe(FantPt ~ ., data = player_train) %>%
  update_role(name, year, college_team, nfl_team, new_role = "ID")


xg_mod <- boost_tree(
  trees = tune(), 
  learn_rate = tune(),
  tree_depth = tune(), 
  min_n = tune(),
  loss_reduction = tune(), 
  sample_size = tune(), 
  mtry = tune(), 
  ) %>% 
  set_mode("regression") %>% 
  set_engine("xgboost")


xg_workflow <- 
  workflow() %>% 
  add_model(xg_mod) %>% 
  add_recipe(player_recipe)


xgb_grid <- grid_latin_hypercube(
  trees(),
  tree_depth(),
  min_n(),
  loss_reduction(),
  sample_size = sample_prop(),
  finalize(mtry(), player_train),
  learn_rate()
)

player_folds <- vfold_cv(player_train)

xgb_res <- tune_grid(
  xg_workflow,
  resamples = player_folds,
  grid = xgb_grid,
  control = control_grid(save_pred = TRUE)
)

best_rmse <- select_best(xgb_res, "rmse")

final_xgb <- finalize_workflow(
  xg_workflow,
  best_rmse
)

xg_fit <- 
  final_xgb %>% 
  fit(data = player_train)

xgpredict <- 
  xg_fit %>% 
  predict(new_data = player_train) %>%
  bind_cols(player_train) 


metrics(xgpredict, FantPt, .pred)

And behold! This table shows my predictions for the top rookie wide receiers for next year. To get here, I looked at total receptions, total yards, total yards per reception, rating, and fan points, all under the XG Boost engine.

1) Jordan Addison - Minnesota Vikings

With my machine, rookie Jordan Addison is expected to have the best season between the rookie receivers. His predicted fantasy points are at 109.5, which isn’t too shabby for a first rounder.

2) Jayden Reed - Green Bay Packers

In the number two spot sits Reed from Michigan State. Reed was the 50th pick in the 2023 draft and is expected to have 97.5 fantasy points.

3) Jaxon Smith-Njigba - Seattle Seahawks

The much-anticipated Smith-Njigba sits at number three in my fantasy point rankings. While at Ohio State, he put up impressive numbers that will be beneficial for the Seahawks, and he’s predicted to bring in 95.6 for a fantasy team.

4) Jalin Hyatt - New York Giants

The Tennessee product headed to the Giants is the lowest draft pick on my list, but don’t count him out. Right now, he’s expected to have 94.5 fantasy points, but his work with the Vols proves he’s a solid receiver.

5) Quentin Johnston - Los Angeles Chargers

Last on my list is Quentin Johnston, the rookie out of TCU. Johnston was the first pick out of TCU and expected to have 94.4 fantasy points this season. The Chargers lack speed on their offense and it’s believed Johnston can be a huge asset to help the Chargers and Herbert.
